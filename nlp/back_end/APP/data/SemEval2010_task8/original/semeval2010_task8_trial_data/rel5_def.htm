Relation 5: Entity-Origin

Preamble

Moldovan et al. (2004) have a relation "SOURCE/FROM" defined as "a place where an entity comes from" (e.g., "olive oil" or "I got it from China"). Levi (1979) has FROM as one of her relations; it refers to a source and acts as an ablative, e.g., "olive oil", "test-tube baby", "apple seed", "rural visitors". Warren (1978) has an Origin-OBJ class (e.g., "seafood"), which is a sub-class of her top-level class Location. FrameNet also has a frame Origin, where an Entity begins its existence in the context of an Origin, which can be a place, culture, text etc.:  http://framenet.icsi.berkeley.edu/index.php?option=com_wrapper&Itemid=118&frame=Origin.

Definition

Entity-Origin(X, Y) is true for a sentence S that mentions the entities X and Y if and only if


(1) S, X and Y are in accordance with the general annotation guidelines (see document http://docs.google.com/Doc?docid=dfhkmm46_0f63mfvf7)


(2) the situation described in S entails that Y is the origin of an entity X (rather than just its location), and X is coming or derived from that origin.

Definition – Restrictions

(a)  The Origin can be:

  1. abstract (e.g., "intuitions/X from mathematics/Y"),

  2. physical (spatial/geographical) location (e.g., "storm/X from the desert/Y") 

  3. material (e.g., "peanut/Y butter/X")

  4. a person or company (e.g. "tablets/Y from a dentist/X")
  5. temporal (e.g., "homes/X from a simpler time/Y").

Most of these categories are subject to specific restrictions to delimit Entity-Origin from potentially overlapping relations,

which are made precise by the following restrictions.


(b) As for the Entity X, it should

  1. NOT be at the Origin (in the case of spatial origins),

  2. NOT consist exclusively of the Origin (in the case of material origins); equivalently, it should have undergone
    substantial processing in order to produce the entity X. As a result, "tobacco/Y ash/X" and "peanut/Y butter/X"
    are positive examples (because the ash does not consist of tobacco any more), but "aluminium/Y plane/X" is a near miss.

  3. The entity X should NOT be part of the origin Y, e.g., "apple/Y seed/X", when the "seed" is separated from the "apple".
    This restriction is meant to prevent overlaps with Component-Whole.

  4. BUT the bulk of X should be made up from products of Y. "rye/Y whiskey/X" is a positive example, while "apple/Y pancake/X" is a
    negative one (because the apples are not the main source for making the cake) .


(c) As for the Origin Y,

  1. Y should NOT be actively involved in the process of bringing the entity X into existence or making it available (e.g., "light/X bulb/Y"
    -- Cause-Effect, "silk/X worm/Y" -- Producer-Product). This restriction is meant to prevent overlaps with Product-Producer and Cause-Effect.
    This is often particularly difficult to ascertain for person/company Origins. The presence of "producer-like" verbs (manufacture, generate) or
    "causer-like" verbs (cause, bring about) is often good evidence against the use of Entity-Origin

  2. To resolve a class of borderline cases, we consider objects emitting radiation/heat/light... as Causers of such emissions, not just Origins.

(d) An entity can have several origins, and each of them separately will count as an origin.


Definition - Overlaps

Potentially overlapping relations:


 * Component-Whole: distinguished by restriction (b.3)

 * Product-Producer, Cause-Effect: distinguished by restrictions (c.1) and (c.2)

 * Communication-Topic: Only a case of apparent overlap. In contrast to Communication-Topic, Entity-Origin describes the relationship

    between the communication and the communicator, rather than between the communication and the topic

    ("news on the hostage crisis").

* Other: for locations (restriction b.1), materials (restriction b.2), flavours/types (restriction b.4)


Positive Examples

"Under state law, minors are not permitted to have <e1>grain</e1> <e2>alcohol</e2>, even if a parent provides it to their children."

Entity-Origin(e2, e1)

Comment: This is a prototypical example of a material Entity-Origin relation. Restriction (b.4) applies.


"Upon first bite Trahyn found that the cake was made with <e1>bacon</e1> <e2>grease</e2>. "

Entity-Origin(e2, e1)

Comment: This is a prototypical example of a material Entity-Origin relation.


"Scientists have also linked the recent UK epidemic to a <e1>desert</e1> <e2>storm</e2> from the Sahara."

Entity-Origin(e2, e1)

Comment: Here the storm has a material origin: the desert.


"One thing SETI has agreed on is that if a <e1>message</e1> from <e2>outer space</e2> is discovered, once it is confirmed by scientists from outside of the organization to authenticate the source, the message will be made known to the public right away."

Entity-Origin(e1, e2)

Comment: This is a prototypical example of a spatial/geographical Entity-Origin relation.


"<e1>The word 'naughtiness'</e1> is derived from a <e2>word</e2> meaning worthless."

Entity-Origin(e1, e2)

Comment: This is a prototypical example of an abstract Entity-Origin relation.

Near-miss Negative Examples

"The temple claims to have the only <e1>orange</e1> <e2>tree</e2> in Paro, and the picking of fruit from the tree is strictly prohibited."

Product-Producer(e1,e2)

Comment: The sentence violates definition restriction (c.1). Here the tree is actively involved in bringing the oranges into existence, and does not serve as a raw material. This is a Product-Producer relation.

 

"Others note that <e1>apple</e1> <e2>seeds</e2> found in Anatolia were carbon dated 6500 BCE."

Component-Whole(e2,e1)

Comment: The sentence violates definition restriction (b.3). This is Component-Whole, i.e. "The seeds used to be part of the apple". In fact, probably only the seeds were found.


"Upon prior <e1>statement</e1> from the <e2>President</e2> in case of reasons of emergency, national security, or public interest, and solely while such reasons remains, the State may subject the patent to a compulsory license at any time and, in such case, the National Bureau of Industrial Property may grant the requested licenses, regardless of the patent holder's right to compensation..."
Product-Producer(e1,e2)

Comment: The sentence violates definition restriction (c.1) where the origin should NOT be actively involved in the process of bringing the entity into existence; this is Product-Produce.

 

"The return to Earth early Monday of the Shenzhou VI with two astronauts on board marked the end of stage one in China's <e1>space</e1> <e2>odyssey</e2>."

Other(e1,e2)

Comment: In this sentence, the space is not the origin, but the destination or the location of the odyssey (cf. restriction b.1). We don't have a relation for this.


"Eventually the <e1>company</e1> manufactured the <e2>machines</e2>."

Product-Producer(e2,e1)

Comment: The sentence violates definition restriction (c.1). This is a Product-Producer relationship.

"How can you prove that a <e1>gas</e1> takes up <e2>space</e2>?"

Other(e1,e2)

Comment: The space is not the origin for the gas. This is none of our present relations.

"A person infected with a particular <e1>flu</e1> <e2>virus</e2> strain develops an antibody against that virus."
Cause-Effect(e2,e1)
Comment: flu is a state, virus is a causal agent. This violates restriction (c.1), and this is an instance of Cause-Effect.
